Sparse vs Dense — Layout Decision
Sparse · Impact & Emotion
Brand awareness, campaign graphics, athlete name reveals. Max 3 competing elements. Dominant negative space is intentional — do not fill it. One metric maximum.
Dense · Data & Analysis
Performance reports, combine results, proposals, dashboards. Every zone works. Clear hierarchy: headline → metrics → supporting data → source. Numbers are visual anchors.
Universal Rules — Apply to All Captions
Lead with the number, never the label  ·  Max 3 lines of copy  ·  No exclamation marks  ·  No adjectives that aren't data ("incredible" means nothing, "10.3" means everything)  ·  Athlete's name is always the last or only attribution line  ·  AMSC never claims the result — the athlete does  ·  No motivational filler: no "beast mode", "crushing it", "on fire"

The Core Rule
Photography must honour the athlete's story, not illustrate AMSC's services. The athlete is always the protagonist. AMSC is always the system behind them.
The Three Photography Modes
Mode 1 — Hero
The athlete IS the content
Full bleed photo, dark base, athlete facing camera or in peak action.
Treatment: High contrast, dark vignette on lower third, subject sharp.
Text placement: Bottom-weighted — name large, metrics in footer bar.
Use for: Athlete spotlights, high-profile event features, credibility posts.
Example: Derrick Ogechi result post.
Mode 2 — Atmosphere
The photo sets the tone. Type tells the story.
Full bleed photo treated as background — darker, less detail.
Treatment: Dark overlay 40–60% opacity black, subject may be partially obscured.
Text placement: Dominant, left-aligned, large display type over photo.
Use for: Brand awareness, campaign content, motivational posts.
Example: "Built for Speed" campaign post.
Mode 3 — Documentary
Raw training footage. Real over polished.
Training environment photos — tracks, courts, gyms, outdoor sessions.
Treatment: Minimal — colour grade only, no heavy overlays.
Text placement: Anchored bottom or side panel, clean data zone.
Use for: Ongoing work content, process posts, behind-the-scenes.
Example: Simani block start training content.
Composition Rules
Framing must be intentional — crop communicates the moment's energy. Block start = coil and tension. Post-game = exhaustion and triumph. Training = focus and discipline.
Never crop an athlete's face or dominant body line without clear artistic intent.
If the photo background is busy — apply a dark vignette or panel to isolate the athlete.
The AMSC logo must always have a clean zone. If the photo bleeds into the logo position — add a subtle dark gradient or relocate the logo.
Data shown must match the photo moment — combine photo from a night match with stats from that match. Mismatched data and photo context destroys credibility.

THE ATHLETE IS ALWAYS THE PROTAGONIST
AMSC is the coach in the corner. Every design decision must honour this. The athlete's name is always larger than the AMSC logo. The achievement is always the headline. AMSC's role is always framing, never competing. Data and metrics belong to the athlete, not the brand.

Red Rules — What Red Hits
Eyebrow labels and category tags
The single most important metric per layout — one number, one layout
Divider lines used as accent bars (2–4px max)
Performance deltas and improvement indicators ▲ +12%
One CTA element per layout maximum
Quote left-border indicator (3px left border on blockquotes)
Red Never Appears On
Large background fills — red is an accent, not a surface
Body text — body is always off-white or grey on dark
Decorative elements with no functional purpose
Multiple competing elements on the same layout
Logo Rules
Always present · Always legible · Always quiet · Never competing
Social: top-left or top-right corner · Documents: bottom-left or bottom-right
Social max width: ~15–20% of canvas · Presentation max: ~10–15% of slide
Never place logo over a busy photo zone without a dark gradient or contrast fix
Athlete's name is always larger than the AMSC logo — no exceptions
Quality Check — Before Finalising Any Output
01Can I identify the most important element within 2 seconds?
02Is the athlete clearly the protagonist?
03Is the AMSC logo legible but not competing?
04Is red used in exactly one dominant zone?
05Does this feel like it belongs next to Altis, EXOS, or Momentous?
06Would this embarrass or elevate the AMSC brand?
