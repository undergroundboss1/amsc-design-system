Logo Usage
Two marks only. The full wordmark (AMSC PERFORMANCE) is primary. The C-stopwatch icon is the standalone symbol — use it as a watermark, app/profile mark, or where the full wordmark won't fit.
Colour is locked to two versions. Red (#a60a08) on dark backgrounds. Off-white (#f5f5f8) on red bars or busy photography. Never any other colour. Never a gradient.
Clear space. Keep clear space around the wordmark equal to the height of the "C" icon. Nothing — text, photo edges, other logos — intrudes into that zone.
Minimum size. Wordmark never below 90px wide on screen. Icon never below 24px. Below that, legibility breaks.
Never stretch, rotate, recolour outside the two approved versions, add effects (shadow/glow/bevel), or place the red mark on a red or mid-tone background where contrast fails.
The logo is always the quietest element in athlete content — present, never competing. See the Athlete Protagonist rule.

The Master Principle — The Red Budget
EVERY LAYOUT GETS ONE DOMINANT RED MOMENT
And only one. The dominant red is the thing the eye must land on first. It is either a red-backed headline highlight, or a single hero metric in red, or one CTA element — never two on the same layout.
Pick ONE dominant per layout

Structural red — quiet, functional, does NOT count against the budget
Thin bars / dividers 2–4px Eyebrow label chips Card top-borders Quote left-borders Deltas ▲ +12%
The squint test: if you squint and more than one red element fights to be "the point," you've overspent. Pull one back to structural, or remove it.
The Core Rule
THE ATHLETE IS ALWAYS THE PROTAGONIST
AMSC is the coach in the corner. Every design decision must honour this. The athlete's name is always larger than the AMSC logo. The achievement is always the headline. AMSC's role is always framing, never competing.
Signature Elements — The AMSC Look
01Red Horizontal Bars as Structure
Narrow, contained, full-width red bars are approved as structural dividers and label-holders — the tagline strip, eyebrow bars, section separators. Height stays limited (a strip, never a panel). This is structural red: it organises, it doesn't compete. Red as a large background surface is still forbidden.
02Red-Backed Headline Highlight
BUILT AROUND YOUR SQUAD.
The strongest device in the system. One key phrase sits on a solid red bar inside the headline. This is the dominant red moment — it expresses the budget, it doesn't break it. One highlight per layout, on the single most important phrase.
03Stopwatch Watermark
The C-stopwatch icon, large and faded (4–8% opacity), bleeding off an edge of a brand frame. Institutional weight without competing for attention. One per layout, behind the content, never overlapping focal text. Full spec under Logos → Icon as Watermark.
04Founder Portrait on the Close
Arnold's portrait is permitted on closing and contact frames only — the human signature behind the institution. Never on content, proof, or methodology slides. It signals there is a real person behind the system at the moment of the ask.
Optional Treatment — Use With Judgement
Red Card Top-Border
A red top-border on cards (as on this card) is optional, not default. Use it when a card grid is the hero element of the layout. Skip it when the dominant red is needed elsewhere — a grid of cards with red top-borders plus a red headline highlight overspends the budget.

Photo Treatment Modes
Mode 1 · Hero
Full-bleed athlete portrait. Subject sharp and dominant. Dark vignette on lower third for text legibility.
Mode 2 · Atmosphere
Athlete partially abstracted. Dark overlay. Large negative space for typography to breathe.
Mode 3 · Documentary
Raw training aesthetic. Unposed. Authentic moment. Minimal treatment.
Every photo template must accept all three modes. Overlay and text zones must hold regardless of treatment.
Signature Grade — Selective Desaturation
HERO IN COLOUR. EVERYONE ELSE FADES BACK.
The AMSC cover grade: the hero athlete stays in full colour while surrounding players are desaturated to near-black-and-white. It isolates the protagonist and carries the protagonist rule into the photography itself. Use on covers and athlete spotlights where one figure must dominate a busy frame.
This is a photo-edit technique, not a layout rule — it is applied in the grade before the image enters a template. Do not attempt to simulate it with overlays in layout. When the hero cannot be cleanly isolated, fall back to Mode 1 Hero with a dark vignette instead.
Photography Principles
Real athletes, real moments. No stock imagery in proof or athlete content.
Dark always wins — favour low-key, high-contrast images that sit on black.
Leave negative space for typography — shoot and crop with the text zone in mind.
Avoid bright, evenly-lit, light-background images — they fight the system.
